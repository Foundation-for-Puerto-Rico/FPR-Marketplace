# FPR 2 CFR Part 200 Compliance Skill

Comprehensive reference for 2 CFR Part 200 — Uniform Administrative Requirements, Cost Principles, and Audit Requirements for Federal Awards — tailored to Foundation for Puerto Rico's federal programs.

## FPR Federal Award Context

| Program | Agency | Funding | Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| WCRP | HUD (CDBG-DR via PRDOH) | CDBG-DR | Pass-through subaward |
| Bottom Up DRI | EDA | $5.58M | Direct federal award |
| PULSO | SBA | $1M | Direct federal award |
| Community Innovation Hub | USDA | TBD | Direct federal award |

FPR is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit (EIN 66-0776227). As a non-federal entity receiving federal financial assistance, FPR must comply with 2 CFR Part 200 for all awards issued on or after December 26, 2014, with the 2024 OMB revisions applying to awards issued on or after October 1, 2024.

For WCRP specifically, FPR is a subrecipient of PRDOH (the pass-through entity), which itself is the recipient of HUD CDBG-DR funds. This means FPR must comply with both 2 CFR 200 and HUD/CDBG-DR program-specific requirements.

## Structure of 2 CFR Part 200

The regulation is organized into six subparts and twelve appendices:

**Subpart A** (§§200.0–200.1) — Acronyms and Definitions. Key terms like "allocable," "allowable," "recipient," "subrecipient," "contractor," "period of performance."

**Subpart B** (§§200.100–200.113) — General Provisions. Applicability, exceptions, conflict of interest, mandatory disclosures.

**Subpart C** (§§200.200–200.217) — Pre-Federal Award Requirements. NOFOs, application review, merit review, federal award terms.

**Subpart D** (§§200.300–200.346) — Post Federal Award Requirements. Financial and program management, property standards, procurement, performance and financial reporting, subrecipient monitoring, record retention, remedies for noncompliance, closeout.

**Subpart E** (§§200.400–200.476) — Cost Principles. Basic cost considerations, direct and indirect costs, selected items of cost (§§200.420–200.476).

**Subpart F** (§§200.500–200.521) — Audit Requirements. Single Audit thresholds, audit scope, reporting, management decisions.

For detailed breakdowns, read the reference files in `references/` — see the Reference Files Guide below.

## The 12 Compliance Areas (OMB Compliance Supplement)

These are the areas tested during a Single Audit. When answering compliance questions, map the issue to the relevant area:

1. **Activities Allowed or Unallowed** — Is the activity within the grant's scope?
2. **Allowable Costs/Cost Principles** — Is the cost reasonable, allocable, consistent, and adequately documented? (Subpart E)
3. **Cash Management** — Are drawdowns timed to minimize federal cash on hand? (§200.305)
4. **Eligibility** — Are beneficiaries/participants eligible per program rules?
5. **Equipment and Real Property Management** — Proper tracking, use, and disposition? (§§200.310–200.316)
6. **Matching, Level of Effort, Earmarking** — Are cost-sharing commitments being met?
7. **Period of Performance** — Are costs incurred within the award's start and end dates? (§200.77)
8. **Procurement and Suspension/Debarment** — Are purchases following proper methods? SAM.gov checks? (§§200.317–200.327)
9. **Program Income** — Is income generated from the award properly accounted for? (§200.307)
10. **Reporting** — Financial and performance reports submitted on time? (§§200.328–200.329)
11. **Subrecipient Monitoring** — Proper risk assessment, monitoring, and on-site reviews? (§§200.331–200.333)
12. **Special Tests and Provisions** — Program-specific requirements (e.g., CDBG-DR national objectives, Davis-Bacon for construction).

## Cost Allowability Decision Framework

When the user asks "Can we charge X to the grant?", apply this test (§§200.403–200.405):

**Step 1 — Is it necessary and reasonable?** Would a prudent person have incurred this cost? Is the amount reasonable for what was obtained?

**Step 2 — Is it allocable?** Is the cost incurred specifically for the benefit of the federal award? Can it be assigned to the award with a reasonable degree of accuracy?

**Step 3 — Is it consistent?** Are similar costs treated the same way (direct vs. indirect) across all funding sources? Like costs must receive like treatment.

**Step 4 — Does it conform to limitations?** Check the award terms, agency regulations, and the Selected Items of Cost (§§200.420–200.476) for specific prohibitions or caps.

**Step 5 — Is it adequately documented?** Proper records, timesheets, receipts, and allocation methodology?

**Step 6 — Does it require prior approval?** Check §200.407 for items requiring prior written approval from the awarding agency (e.g., participant support costs, equipment over $10k, foreign travel, pre-award costs).

If the cost passes all six tests → **Allowable**. If it fails any → **Unallowable** (explain which test it fails and cite the specific section).

## Indirect Cost Rate for Nonprofits

FPR can recover indirect costs through one of these methods:

1. **Negotiated Rate** — FPR negotiates a rate with its cognizant agency. Applied to Modified Total Direct Costs (MTDC).
2. **De Minimis Rate** — 15% of MTDC (§200.414(f)), available to organizations that have never had a negotiated rate. The 2024 revision increased this from 10% to 15% for awards issued on/after October 1, 2024.
3. **Cost Allocation Plan** — Alternative approach for organizations with complex cost structures.

**MTDC** excludes: equipment (>$10k), capital expenditures, rental costs of real property, participant support costs, tuition remission, and the portion of subaward expenditures exceeding $25,000 per subaward.

## Procurement Standards (§§200.317–200.327)

FPR must follow these procurement methods based on purchase amount:

| Method | Threshold (2024) | Requirements |
|---|---|---|
| Micro-purchase | ≤$10,000 | No competitive quotes required. Must distribute equitably among qualified suppliers. |
| Simplified acquisition | $10,001–$250,000 | Price/rate quotes from adequate number of sources. Document selection. |
| Sealed bids | >$250,000 (construction) | Formal sealed bid process. Firm fixed-price. Publicly advertised. |
| Competitive proposals | >$250,000 (non-construction) | RFP process. Evaluation criteria. Written evaluation. |
| Noncompetitive (sole source) | Any amount | Only when: item available from single source, public emergency, awarding agency authorizes, or competition deemed inadequate after solicitation. Requires documentation. |

Additional requirements: full and open competition, written conflict-of-interest policies, contractor oversight, preference for small/minority/veteran-owned businesses (§200.321), domestic procurement preferences, SAM.gov verification for suspension/debarment (§200.214).

## Subrecipient vs. Contractor Determination (§200.331)

This distinction is critical for WCRP where FPR works with 17 subrecipient organizations:

**Subrecipient** characteristics: determines eligibility, makes programmatic decisions, uses award to carry out a program, responsible for compliance with program requirements, performance measured against program objectives.

**Contractor** characteristics: provides goods/services as part of normal business, operates in a competitive environment, provides similar goods/services to many purchasers, not subject to program compliance requirements.

The substance of the relationship determines the classification, not what the agreement is called.

## Subrecipient Monitoring Requirements (§§200.332–200.333)

As a pass-through entity for WCRP subrecipients, FPR (or PRDOH, as applicable) must:

1. Assess risk of noncompliance for each subrecipient
2. Include required elements in subaward agreements (CFDA number, award name, period of performance, indirect cost rate, compliance requirements)
3. Monitor subrecipient activities to ensure compliance
4. Verify subrecipients are not suspended/debarred (SAM.gov)
5. Evaluate whether subrecipients met audit requirements (if spending ≥$1M federal)
6. Issue management decisions on audit findings within 6 months
7. Consider adjusting monitoring based on risk assessment results

## Key 2024 Revisions (Effective October 1, 2024)

Awards issued on or after this date must comply with these changes:

- **Audit threshold increased** from $750,000 to $1,000,000 in federal expenditures per fiscal year (§200.501)
- **De minimis indirect rate increased** from 10% to 15% of MTDC (§200.414(f))
- **Simplified acquisition** terminology replaces "small purchases" (§200.320)
- **Veteran-owned businesses** added to procurement preferences alongside small/minority/women-owned (§200.321)
- **Tribal sovereignty** recognized — Indian Tribes may follow own procurement policies (§200.317)
- **Written policies required** — emphasis on documented internal controls, procurement procedures, and compliance policies
- **SEFA** may be prepared under GAAP or other comprehensive basis of accounting (OCBOA)

## Record Retention (§200334)

FPR must retain financial records, supporting documents, statistical records, and all other non-federal entity records pertinent to a federal award for **three years** from the date of submission of the final expenditure report, or for awards renewed quarterly or annually, from the date of the last expenditure report for that period.

Exceptions: records for real property and equipment (retain 3 years after final disposition), records related to audit resolution (retain until resolved), indirect cost rate proposals (retain if submitted for negotiation).

## Program Income (§200.307)

Income earned by FPR that is directly generated by the federal award or earned as a result of the award during the period of performance. Common FPR examples could include: fees for services, sale of commodities, conference registration fees.

Default method: **deduction** (income reduces the federal award amount). But the awarding agency may specify addition (income added to award) or cost sharing (income used to meet matching requirements).

## How to Use This Skill

When answering a compliance question:

1. Identify which of the 12 compliance areas the question falls under
2. Cite the specific 2 CFR 200 section(s)
3. For cost allowability questions, walk through the six-step decision framework
4. Note any FPR-specific considerations (e.g., WCRP is a CDBG-DR subaward with additional HUD requirements)
5. Flag if the question requires reading a reference file for more detail
6. Always caveat that this is regulatory guidance, not legal advice — FPR should consult with its grants management team or legal counsel for binding interpretations
7. When relevant, note whether the 2024 revisions change the answer
