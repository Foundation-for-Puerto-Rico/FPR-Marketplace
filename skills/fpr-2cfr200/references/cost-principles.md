## 1. Basic Considerations

### Composition of Costs (§200.400)
Total cost of a federal award = direct costs + allocable portion of indirect costs - applicable credits (refunds, rebates, discounts).

### Factors Affecting Allowability (§200.403)
A cost is allowable only if ALL of these are met:
- Necessary and reasonable for the award's performance
- Allocable to the award under the principles in this subpart
- Conforms to any limitations or exclusions in the award, Subpart E, or agency regulations
- Consistent with policies and procedures that apply uniformly to both federally-financed and other activities
- Accorded consistent treatment (a cost cannot be direct on one award and indirect on another if circumstances are similar)
- Determined in accordance with GAAP (except as otherwise provided)
- Not included as a cost or used to meet cost sharing/matching for another federal award (no double-dipping)
- Adequately documented

### Reasonable Costs (§200.404)
A cost is reasonable if, in its nature and amount, it does not exceed what a prudent person would pay under the same or similar circumstances. Consider:
- Whether the cost is ordinary and necessary for the operation
- Market prices for comparable goods or services in the geographic area
- Whether individuals acted with prudence under the circumstances
- Whether the entity significantly deviated from its established practices

### Allocable Costs (§200.405)
A cost is allocable if it is incurred specifically for the federal award, benefits both the award and other work (and can be distributed in reasonable proportion), or is necessary to the overall operation and is assignable to the award per the cost allocation principles.

If a cost benefits two or more projects or activities, it must be allocated proportionally. A cost allocable to a particular federal award may not be charged to other federal awards to overcome fund deficiencies, avoid restrictions, or for other reasons of convenience.

## 2. Direct vs. Indirect Costs

### Direct Costs (§200.413)
Costs that can be identified specifically with a particular final cost objective (i.e., the federal award). Examples for FPR:
- Salaries of staff working directly on a program (with time documentation)
- Travel directly related to the program
- Supplies purchased for program activities
- Consultant costs for program-specific work
- Subrecipient costs (subawards)

Direct charging of administrative/clerical staff salaries is allowable only when: (a) integral to the program, (b) individuals can be specifically identified with the project, (c) explicitly included in the budget or prior approval obtained, and (d) costs are not also recovered as indirect.

### Indirect (F&A) Costs (§200.414)
Costs incurred for a common or joint purpose benefitting more than one cost objective, not readily assignable to a specific award without effort disproportionate to the results achieved. Examples:
- Executive leadership and general administration
- Accounting, HR, legal
- Office rent and utilities (shared spaces)
- IT infrastructure (shared)
- Depreciation on shared equipment

### The Consistency Rule
Once a cost is treated as direct, similar costs in similar circumstances must always be treated as direct. Same for indirect. Switching treatment without a legitimate, documented reason creates audit risk.

## 3. Modified Total Direct Costs (MTDC)

MTDC = All direct salaries and wages, applicable fringe benefits, materials and supplies, services, travel, and up to the first $25,000 of each subaward.

**MTDC Exclusions:**
- Equipment (items with unit cost ≥$10,000 and useful life >1 year)
- Capital expenditures
- Charges for patient care
- Rental costs of real property and equipment
- Tuition remission
- Scholarships and fellowships
- Participant support costs
- The portion of each subaward in excess of $25,000

This is the base to which the indirect cost rate (negotiated or de minimis 15%) is applied.

## 4. Selected Items of Cost — Full Reference (§§200.420–200.476)

### Always Allowable (with documentation)
- **Compensation — personal services** (§200.430): Salaries proportional to work performed. Requires time documentation.
- **Compensation — fringe benefits** (§200.431): Social security, retirement, health insurance, workers' comp, leave, etc.
- **Materials and supplies** (§200.453): Purchased items consumed in performing the award.
- **Professional service costs** (§200.459): Consultants, legal, audit services with documented rationale.
- **Communication costs** (§200.420): Phone, internet, postage, mail when directly allocable.
- **Insurance/indemnification** (§200.447): Required coverage for operations.
- **Maintenance and repair** (§200.452): Keep capital assets in efficient condition.
- **Printing and reproduction** (§200.461): Required for program activities.
- **Training and education** (§200.473): Employee development related to the award.
- **Transportation costs** (§200.474): Freight, express, cartage.
- **Travel costs** (§200.475): Per organizational policy. Domestic = straightforward. Foreign = prior approval unless in budget.

### Conditionally Allowable (restrictions apply)
- **Advertising and public relations** (§200.421): Recruitment, procurement, program outreach OK. Image/PR not allowable.
- **Advisory councils** (§200.422): Allowable if authorized by statute, the award, or agency.
- **Conferences and meetings** (§200.432): Allowable if purpose directly benefits the program. No entertainment.
- **Equipment and other capital expenditures** (§200.439): >$10,000 per unit requires prior approval. Must be in budget or separately approved.
- **Memberships, subscriptions, professional activity** (§200.454): Business/professional organizations OK. Lobbying organizations not allowable.
- **Participant support costs** (§200.456): Prior approval required. Not included in MTDC. Stipends, subsistence, travel for participants.
- **Proposal costs** (§200.460): Preparing proposals for future awards. Normally treated as indirect.
- **Publication and scholarship** (§200.461): Allowable for disseminating research findings.
- **Rearrangement and reconversion** (§200.462): Moving costs, facility rearrangement. Prior approval may be needed.
- **Recruiting costs** (§200.463): Short-term, special recruitment efforts. Relocation costs are conditional.
- **Taxes** (§200.470): Allowable except those the entity is exempt from (FPR is tax-exempt under IRC §501(c)(3)).

### Always Unallowable
- **Alcoholic beverages** (§200.423): Never.
- **Bad debts** (§200.426): Never.
- **Contingency provisions** (§200.433): Contributions to contingency reserves.
- **Entertainment costs** (§200.438): Unless specifically authorized.
- **Fines, penalties, damages** (§200.441): Resulting from violations.
- **Fundraising and investment management** (§200.442): Raising capital, organized fundraising.
- **General government expenses** (§200.444): Not applicable to nonprofits.
- **Goods or services for personal use** (§200.445): Personal items.
- **Lobbying** (§200.450): Influencing legislation, executive orders, or outcomes of elections.
- **Losses on other awards** (§200.451): Can't charge Award A to cover losses on Award B.
- **Selling and marketing** (§200.467): Costs of selling products or services.

## 5. Time-and-Effort Documentation (§200.430)

For FPR staff charged to federal awards, the organization must maintain a system that:

- Accounts for total activity (100% of effort, not just federally-funded portion)
- Reflects after-the-fact distribution of actual activity (not budgeted or planned)
- Accounts for all funding sources (federal, non-federal, unfunded)
- Is supported by a system of internal controls that provides reasonable assurance charges are accurate, allowable, and properly allocated

The 2024 revisions removed the prescriptive requirements for semi-annual certifications and monthly personnel activity reports (PARs) that existed in the old OMB circulars. Instead, the standard is now principles-based — the organization's system must meet the criteria above, whatever form it takes.

FPR should have a documented time-and-effort policy that specifies:
- How employees record their time
- Frequency of reporting (biweekly, monthly)
- Who reviews and approves time records
- How corrections are handled
- How split-funded employees allocate effort

## 6. Cost Allocation Methods

When costs benefit multiple programs, FPR needs a documented cost allocation methodology:

**Direct allocation** — Costs charged entirely to one award when they clearly benefit only that award.

**Proportional allocation** — Costs split among awards based on a reasonable and documented basis (e.g., FTEs, square footage, direct costs, headcount).

**Cost allocation plan** — Formal plan documenting how shared costs are distributed. Submitted to the cognizant agency if required.

Common allocation bases for FPR:
- Staff FTE percentages (for shared personnel)
- Direct program costs (for administrative overhead)
- Square footage (for shared office space)
- Usage logs (for shared equipment, vehicles)

## 7. Prior Approval Requirements (§200.407)

The following items require prior written approval from the awarding agency before charging to the award. For WCRP, this means approval from PRDOH and/or HUD:

- Budget revisions involving changes between cost categories exceeding the threshold in the award (typically 10% of total budget)
- Change in scope or objectives of the project
- Need for additional federal funding
- Transfer of budget amounts for participant support costs
- Capital expenditures (including equipment >$10,000)
- Foreign travel (unless specifically in the approved budget)
- Changes in key personnel
- Pre-award costs (costs incurred before the period of performance)
- No-cost extensions (extending the period of performance)
- Subawards not in the approved budget
- Program income earned during the award
- Transfer of funds between budget categories (if award restricts)

## 8. FPR-Specific Considerations

### Multiple Federal Funding Sources
FPR receives awards from HUD (CDBG-DR), EDA, SBA, and USDA. Each agency may have program-specific requirements that layer on top of 2 CFR 200. Staff working across programs need time documentation showing effort on each award.

### Cost Sharing and Matching
Some FPR awards may require matching. When documenting match:
- Third-party in-kind contributions must be documented and valued at fair market value
- Volunteer time must be documented with timesheets
- The same costs cannot be used as match on more than one award
- Match must be allowable under the same cost principles as direct charges

### Indirect Costs Strategy
FPR should evaluate whether to:
1. Use the de minimis 15% rate (simplest, no negotiation needed)
2. Negotiate a rate with the cognizant agency (potentially higher recovery)
3. Use a cost allocation plan

The choice depends on FPR's administrative cost structure and whether 15% adequately covers indirect costs. Once elected, the de minimis rate must be used consistently for all federal awards until a negotiated rate is obtained.

### WCRP Pass-Through Considerations
For WCRP, FPR operates as both a subrecipient (of PRDOH) and potentially as a pass-through entity (to the 17 subrecipient organizations). This dual role means FPR must:
- Comply as a subrecipient (follow PRDOH's terms, submit reports, cooperate with monitoring)
- Comply as a pass-through (monitor its own subrecipients, assess risk, ensure audit compliance)
